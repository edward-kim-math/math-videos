When multiplying fractions, there is no requirement for a common denominator.
To multiply fractions, we just multiply straight across.
Let's look at an example. What is two fifths times three sevenths?
To multiply fractions, we multiply the numerators together and the denominators together.
Simplifying the top and bottom, our single fraction is six over thirty-five.
Let's look at another example. What is one tenth times one sixth?
As a reminder, for multiplying fractions, there is no need for a common denominator.
To multiply these fractions, we multiply the numerators together and the denominators together.
Simplifying the top and bottom, our single fraction is one over sixty.
Let's look at another example. What is three sevenths times two sevenths?
Notice both fractions have seven as a denominator. That should not change our process. We will still multiply straight across.
We multiply numerators three and two together.
We also multiply the denominators seven and seven together.
Simplifying the numerator and denominator, we have six over forty-nine.
The incorrect belief that you need a common denominator to multiply fractions leads people to get the incorrect final answer of six over seven.
However, we only need a common denominator for adding and subtracting fractions, not multiplying.
The correct answer is six over forty-nine. The denominator of forty-nine is the result of multiplying the first denominator (which is seven) by the second denominator (which is also coincidentally seven).
